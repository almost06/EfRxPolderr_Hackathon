## The questions this jury will actually ask

**Q1 — "Why hasn't Reframe or NeedsList already built this?"**
> Reframe is a directory — you wait to be browsed. NeedsList is for material supplies. No one has built **active, fairness-corrected matching**, and no one used **AI to fix the root cause** — that grassroots leaders can't pitch in the language donors expect. That's new, and it's only buildable now.

**Q2 — "How does it sustain itself financially?"** *(Polderr lens)*
> Free for refugee-led orgs and individual donors — the side with the least capacity never pays. Revenue comes from a small fee on **institutional and corporate** funding flows, plus foundation grants. The barrier stays at zero for the people we serve.

**Q3 — "Isn't the AI putting words in their mouth / inventing impact?"** *(the EfR-dignity trap — answer carefully)*
> No. The AI is explicitly **forbidden from inventing numbers** — if a figure isn't in the notes, it stays qualitative. It professionalizes the organization's **own words**, and the leader reviews and edits before publishing. It removes a language barrier, not authorship. The work and the truth stay theirs.

**Q4 — "What stops someone creating 50 fake organizations?"**
> Trust is layered: independent **multi-party vouching** (established NGOs vouch for RLOs), **privacy-preserving in-region verification**, and behavioral signals. We treat fraud-resistance as a visible trust feature, not an afterthought. Today we already show who vouches for whom.

**Q5 — "Doesn't showing an organization's location endanger refugees?"** *(critical — they may test this)*
> We designed for exactly this. Verification is **private to the platform** — donors only ever see "verified in-region — Kakuma, Kenya," never precise coordinates. Privacy-preserving by design.

**Q6 — "Isn't your ranking just another popularity contest?"**
> The opposite. Our **anti-Matthew layer penalizes recently well-funded orgs** so the chronically under-funded surface higher. We show fit labels — Strong / Good / Complementary — **never scores or a leaderboard**. We refuse to build a ranking.

**Q7 — "What about organizations with no smartphone or internet?"**
> The co-pilot is built to run over **WhatsApp** — a text or voice note from a basic phone, no app, no data plan. That's the lowest-bandwidth intake possible short of paper. Live today on web; WhatsApp is the production channel.

**Q8 — "Who owns the AI-generated profile and the data?"**
> The organization does. It's their content, professionalized — they can edit, publish, unpublish, and take it elsewhere. We're infrastructure, not a gatekeeper that locks them in.

**Q9 — "How big could this realistically get?"** *(Polderr/startup lens)*
> Tens of thousands of refugee-led and grassroots orgs are invisible to a motivated, underserved donor pool — including **diaspora communities** desperate for a trusted channel home. The wedge is energy-for-refugees with EfR's real projects; the same engine generalizes to any cause.

**Q10 — "What did you cut, and what's next?"**
> We deliberately built **one flawless loop** — notes → AI profile → matchable project → donor discovery — instead of many half-features. Next: WhatsApp intake, a vouching web-of-trust, diaspora matching, and milestone-based escrow.

**Q11 — "Could a small NGO actually run and maintain this?"** *(feasibility, 15%)*
> Yes — it's one AI call (under a cent each) and a simple backend with an in-memory database. No ML infrastructure, no large team. A small NGO coalition could operate it on modest hosting.

**Q12 — "What's actually built vs. a mockup?"**
> The matching engine, the equity layer, blind discovery, the portfolio split, two-sided matching, and the AI co-pilot — including publish-to-discoverable — are all **working live**, which you just saw. Vouching/WhatsApp/escrow are the roadmap.
